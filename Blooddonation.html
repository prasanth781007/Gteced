Blood Donation

Saving Lives Through Voluntary Blood Donation

About Blood Donation

A blood donation occurs when a healthy person voluntarily has blood drawn. The blood is used for transfusions or made into medications by a process called fractionation.

Global Perspective

In the developed world, most blood donors are unpaid volunteers who give blood for a community supply. In poorer countries, established supplies are limited and donors usually give blood when family or friends need a transfusion.

Motivation

Many donors donate as an act of charity, but some are paid and in some cases there are incentives other than money such as paid time off from work. A donor can also have blood drawn for their own future use.

Safety

Donating is relatively safe, but some donors have bruising where the needle is inserted or may feel faint. Potential donors are evaluated for anything that might make their blood unsafe to use.

Screening Process

Potential donors go through a thorough screening process to ensure the safety of both donors and recipients:

1

Health History

Donors are asked about medical history and recent travel

2

Physical Check

Short physical examination including temperature, pulse, and blood pressure

3

Hemoglobin Test

Small blood sample tested to ensure adequate iron levels

4

Disease Screening

Testing for diseases that can be transmitted by blood transfusion

Disease Testing

The screening includes testing for diseases that can be transmitted by a blood transfusion, including HIV and viral hepatitis. The donor is also asked about medical history and given a short physical examination to make sure that the donation is not hazardous to his or her health.

How often a donor can give varies from days to months based on what he or she donates and the laws of the country where the donation takes place. For example, in the United States donors must wait 8 weeks (56 days) between whole blood donations but only three days between plateletpheresis donations.

The amount of blood drawn and the methods vary, but a typical donation is 450 milliliters (or approximately one US pint) of whole blood. The collection can be done manually or with automated equipment that only takes specific portions of the blood.

Supply Challenge

Most of the components of blood used for transfusions have a short shelf life, and maintaining a constant supply is a persistent problem.

Benefits of Blood Donation

Health Benefits

Reduces risk of heart disease and helps maintain healthy iron levels

Calorie Burn

Donating blood burns approximately 650 calories per donation

Blood Cell Refresh

Stimulates production of new blood cells

Psychological Benefits

Sense of satisfaction from helping save lives

Eligibility Criteria

Age

Generally between 18-65 years (varies by country)

Weight

Minimum 50 kg (110 lbs) for most blood donations

Health

Should be in good health at the time of donation

Medications

Most medications do not prevent donation
